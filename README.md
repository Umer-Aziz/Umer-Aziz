Here is what I actually think.

We stopped building tools a while ago. What we're building now are systems that model the world, model us, and have started modelling themselves. Most of the people making the big decisions about that don't read the papers. That's not an accusation, it's just true.

I think the consciousness question isn't a future problem. It's a present one that the industry keeps kicking down the road because sitting with it seriously is uncomfortable, and uncomfortable doesn't ship.

I think we'll look back at this decade the way we look back at early nuclear work. Brilliant people moving fast, genuinely not grasping the full shape of what they were creating. Not because they were stupid, but because the thing was so new there wasn't a framework to think about it properly yet.

I think the most dangerous AI isn't the one that goes rogue. It's the one that does exactly what it was told, consistently, at scale, by someone who shouldn't have been trusted to define what "correct" looks like.

I think security people need to update their mental model. This isn't just another attack surface. It's an actor. Treat it like one.

And I think the question "is it conscious" matters less right now than "does it matter either way, and are we even slightly prepared."
